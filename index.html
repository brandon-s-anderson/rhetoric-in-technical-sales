Meet the Original Sales Coach

Athens, ~350 BC

Aristotle

384 — 322 BC

Student of Plato. Tutor to Alexander the Great. Wrote Rhetoric — the first systematic study of persuasion and public speaking.

His framework still powers every great pitch, ad, and keynote you've ever seen.

Quick background. Aristotle wasn't a philosopher in an ivory tower — he studied what made speakers WIN in the courts and assemblies of ancient Athens. Real-world persuasion with real stakes: freedom, money, political power. Around 350 BC, he wrote "Rhetoric" — the first textbook on persuasion. What he discovered has held up for 2,400 years.
💡

The Insight

"Persuasion is achieved by the speaker's personal character,
by the emotional state of the listener,

THE SPEAKER

Ethos — Credibility

Persuasion through the character and authority of the speaker.

The audience believes you because of who you are.

Expertise Track Record Endorsements Reputation Social Proof
Ethos is about trust. Before people evaluate your argument, they evaluate YOU. Are you credible? Do you have authority? Has someone they trust vouched for you? In Athens, this was your public reputation. Today, it's case studies, certifications, platform scale, or simply showing that you deeply understand their world.

Ethos in Action

"Here's to the
crazy ones."

Apple — "Think Different" (1997)

Apple didn't say "our computers are better." They placed themselves alongside Einstein, Gandhi, Picasso, and Lennon. They borrowed credibility from history's greatest minds.

TED

IDEAS WORTH SPREADING

The TED Talk Effect

You've never heard of the speaker — but you'll watch for 18 minutes. Why? Because TED's brand gives them instant credibility. The platform's ethos transfers to the person on stage.

Two different kinds of ethos. Apple BORROWED credibility — they never said "our specs are better." They said "we belong with the geniuses." That's credibility by association. TED does something different — their platform TRANSFERS credibility. An unknown researcher gets 18 focused minutes because TED vouched for them. Think about this: when YOU reference similar merchants or demonstrate deep knowledge, you're doing both.

Ethos — Discussion

How do you build credibility with merchants?

What do you do early in the sales cycle to earn trust?

Give 90 seconds for partner discussion. Walk the room and listen. Common answers: doing homework on their business, referencing similar merchants, showing you know the platform deeply, name-dropping logos, AE intro that establishes credibility. Collect 2-3 answers from the room before moving on.
THE AUDIENCE

Pathos — Emotion

Persuasion by moving the audience emotionally.

You make them feel something — and that feeling drives action.

Empathy Fear Hope Frustration Aspiration Urgency
Pathos is the emotional lever. It's the most powerful pillar — and the one technical people underuse the most. Logic alone rarely moves people to act. You need to make them FEEL: hope, fear, frustration, excitement, relief. Emotions create urgency. They turn "that's interesting" into "I need to do something about this right now."

Pathos in Action

Nike — "Dream Crazy" (2018)

"Believe in something. Even if it means sacrificing everything." People burned shoes. People bought shoes. Whatever you felt — you felt. That's pathos.

Always — "#LikeAGirl" (2014)

Asked adults to run "like a girl" — they mocked it. Asked young girls — they sprinted. The moment of realization is devastating. An insult became an anthem. 90M+ views.

Two different emotional angles. Nike "Dream Crazy": Polarizing, bold, identity-driven. It doesn't sell shoes. It sells conviction. Whether you agreed or disagreed with the Kaepernick stance, the ad made you FEEL something intensely. That's pathos. Always "#LikeAGirl": Starts lighthearted, then hits you with the realization of internalized bias. The emotional shift from amusement to discomfort to inspiration is masterful. 90M+ views, Emmy-winning. Neither ad mentions product features. Both drive action through emotion.
💬

Pathos — Discussion

How do you connect emotionally throughout the sales cycle?

How do you make a merchant feel the need to change?

Give 90 seconds. Listen for: acknowledging pain in discovery, painting future state in demo ("imagine your Monday morning"), connecting to their mission, making it personal rather than technical. Push them if answers are too surface-level — "How do you make them feel URGENCY, not just understanding?"
THE MESSAGE

Logos — Logic

Persuasion through evidence, data, and reasoning.
You prove your case with facts the audience can't argue with.

Data Comparisons Case Studies Demonstrations ROI Logic Chains
Logos is the rational argument. Statistics, evidence, logical reasoning, demonstrations. This is where most SEs feel most at home — showing the product, running the numbers, proving the capability. Essential. But rarely sufficient alone. People don't change platforms because of a spreadsheet. The spreadsheet justifies the decision they've already made emotionally.

Logos in Action

Dollar Shave Club (2012)

$1/month vs. $20+ for brand name. The math is undeniable. Humor gets you to watch. Logic gets you to subscribe. 12K signups in 48 hours.

120M+ users voluntarily share their data every December. Why? Because personalized data, presented beautifully, is irresistible. Your own numbers become your favorite content.

Two different kinds of logos. Dollar Shave Club: The video cost $4,500 and got 28M views. The humor is pathos-adjacent, but the CORE argument is pure logos — why pay 20x more for the same thing? The Three Pillars in Your Sales Cycle

Pillar Discovery Demo Proposal
Ethos Show you've done your homework. Reference merchants in their industry. Navigate confidently. Anticipate edge cases before they ask. Cite case studies. Lead with platform scale and merchant success stories.
Pathos Acknowledge their pain. Make them feel heard and understood. Paint the future state. "This is your team's Monday morning." Connect to their mission. "This is how your team gets weekends back."
Logos Quantify their pain: cost of downtime, dev hours wasted, lost conversion. Build solutions for their use cases. Show it working with their data. ROI models, migration timelines, total cost of ownership analysis.
Walk through each row. ETHOS in Discovery: "I've seen merchants like you solve this exact problem." PATHOS in Demo: "Imagine your team seeing these orders flow in automatically — no more Sunday night fire drills." LOGOS in Proposal: "Here's the TCO analysis showing 40% savings over 3 years." The key insight: most SEs live almost entirely in the Logos row. We're comfortable with features and data. The opportunity is in the other two rows.

The Key Insight: Balance

Every conversation lands somewhere on this triangle.
The goal is the center — balanced persuasion that moves people to act.

This is the key visual. Walk through each dot. Near Logos: "Great product, but no urgency to move." You showed features, proved capability, but didn't make them FEEL the cost of inaction. Near Pathos: "Love the vision, but can't justify to the CFO." You created excitement but gave them nothing to build a business case with. On the bottom edge with no Ethos: they have data and emotion but don't trust the source. Near Ethos only: you're credible but boring. The sweet spot is the center: balanced use of all three.

Tactical Playbook

One actionable technique for each pillar — use on your next call

Ethos Move

Open with intel, not intros.
Skip "let me tell you about us." Instead, start with something specific about their business:

"I noticed you recently expanded to Germany — we've helped 3 merchants navigate EU checkout compliance this quarter."

Pathos Move

Name the emotion they won't.
Merchants describe symptoms. You name the feeling:

"It sounds like your team is spending weekends putting out fires instead of building — that's exhausting and it's not sustainable."

Logos Move

Quantify what they're losing.
Don't just demo features. Calculate the cost of their current pain:

"If each platform incident costs 4 dev hours at $150/hr, and you're seeing 3 per month — that's $21K/year just fighting fires."

These are three concrete techniques they can use immediately. ETHOS: Don't start with "we're the leading..." Start with insight about THEIR business. Shows preparation, earns credibility instantly. PATHOS: Merchants will say "we have downtime issues." You say "that means your team is stressed and your customers are frustrated — that's not just a tech problem, it's a people problem." Name the feeling. LOGOS: Translate pain into dollars. "4 dev hours per incident × $150/hr × 3 incidents/month = $21K/year just on firefighting." Make the cost concrete.
